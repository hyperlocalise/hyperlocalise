## Design Context

### Users
Hyperlocalise's primary web audience is localization managers evaluating new workflow infrastructure for multilingual product delivery. They are likely reviewing tools in a work context, comparing operational confidence, integration fit, and review workflow maturity before committing to a conversation or joining the waitlist.

The immediate job to be done for the site is to help these users understand the product quickly, trust its operational model, and convert to the waitlist.

### Brand Personality
The brand should feel innovative, trustworthy, and cultural.

Emotionally, the interface should feel confident and calm rather than loud or sales-heavy. It should communicate that Hyperlocalise is technically forward-looking while still feeling reliable enough for teams responsible for release quality across languages.

### Aesthetic Direction
The visual direction should take cues from Vercel's clarity and composure: sharp structure, strong hierarchy, polished restraint, and high perceived quality. Hyperlocalise should not look flashy, noisy, or overly colorful.

The product should support both light and dark mode. In both themes, use controlled contrast, disciplined accents, and a refined palette rather than bright promotional color. The visual language should suggest modern infrastructure with cultural sensitivity, not generic developer minimalism and not playful consumer SaaS branding.

Anti-reference: avoid aesthetics that feel overly flashy, trend-chasing, rainbow-heavy, or decorative without purpose.

### Design Principles
1. Build trust before spectacle. Every visual choice should reinforce operational credibility and release confidence.
2. Keep the interface calm under complexity. Present sophisticated localization workflows with structure, clarity, and composure.
3. Use innovation as precision, not noise. The product should feel advanced because it is thoughtful and sharp, not because it is visually loud.
4. Respect multilingual nuance. Design should feel globally aware and culturally literate rather than generic or monocultural.
5. Maintain restrained polish across both light and dark themes, with accents used sparingly and intentionally.
